Jumpstart

Bridging the career knowledge gap.

It’s common for students and early-career professionals to feel at a loss as to how to build their careers.

Jumpstart is a web app that uses a chatbot to help them navigate their start in the professional world.

The Problem

Across the nation, institutions vary widely in the types of resources and connections they can provide. In some institutions the advisement ratio is as poor as 1:1600. Job seekers struggle to find work in an increasingly competitive job market. How can we bring students and early-career professionals the information they need and level the playing field?

The Solution

Jumpstart uses a chatbot to connect users with career development content depending on their level of experience. The chatbot will assess where users are in their careers, learn more about their goals, and then recommend resources from the web and Jumpstart’s Career Guide. The Career Guide is a curated collection of high quality content about career development, including networking, resumes and cover letters, interviewing, salary negotiation, and more.

"Hi there! Welcome to Jumpstart. I’m Vita, an AI powered chatbot that’ll help you build your dream career."

Research and Discovery

User Survey

I surveyed 46 people on their frustrations with their career development. Some key insights gained include:

  • 71% of students and 56% of early-career professionals wish they learned more practical information about the professional world, such as resume writing and interview practice.
  • 71% of students and 81% of early-career professionals said that the first method they use to look for answers to career-related questions was a Google Search.
  • Participants expressed frustration with the process of finding answers to their career questions, saying that advice online comes from people with a variety of different backgrounds - it can be difficult to figure out what’s best for yourself.

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis on products from three companies in the employment industry: LinkedIn, CareerBuilder, and Indeed. I learned about what each product does well, and more importantly, what they don’t do well.

I identified two opportunities for a new competitor.

  • Curation. There is a glut of career information out there. How’s a busy person to know what content is worth their time and attention?
  • Entry-level is underserved. Not all job seekers have the same needs, but each of the products I analyzed is built like they do. How can we provide early-career users with a better experience?

User Personas

I interviewed three users who participated in the survey and condensed insights from those interviews to build user personas. Recurring emotions and pain points from the interviews include:

  • feeling like everyone else knows what they’re doing, especially when it comes to in-person networking
  • struggling to make free time, let alone make time to contemplate the future
  • talking to career services in person tends to be a last resort
  • amount of information online is overwhelming

The users tend to fall into three categories:

  • Students early in their education - figuring out their intended majors and career path, very new to college life
  • Students in later stages of their education - might be looking for internships or even their first jobs in their fields
  • Recent grads or career changers

Main Insights

Users want access to more practical information about the professional world. Because of the immense volume of content on career development that can be found online, it’s frustrating to find quality, actionable information. Even high-quality content is produced by a variety of people with diverse backgrounds, so it can be a challenge for users to determine what applies to them.

Defining a Strategy

From research, I knew that users struggled to find answers to their career-related questions and were overwhelmed by the amount of information online. My rough idea for a solution at this point was to build a career guide website that focuses specifically on practical information that would be helpful to students and early-career professionals. The website would have a chatbot to help curate what is shown to users and connect them with resources to point them in the right direction.

Why would chat work in this scenario? Google’s guidelines on conversation design gives a few guidelines to help designers assess. First, users already have human to human conversations about their careers. Second, users feel comfortable talking or typing about this topic. Third, for an MVP with a chatbot focused on recommending career development content, it is possible for the interaction to be brief, with minimal back-and-forth dialog.

Conversation Design

In my research I discovered that users find it difficult to obtain answers to their career development questions. A majority said that their first instinct is to do a Google Search, but are often overwhelmed by the glut of information in the results. The resources they find are vague or they are unsure if it applies to them. A substantial part of my ideation at this point in the design process was devoted to figuring out how a chatbot could help users solve this problem.

Low Fidelity Wireframes

I now understood enough about user goals to begin wireframing what this product might look like. I began by sketching my ideas for Wardrobe’s screens on paper.

I then built digital wireframes with Figma. Throughout the wireframing process I regularly referred back to Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines to make sure I was designing a great user experience for an iOS app.

I used a card-based design because it chunks content for quick scannability and allows the image-based content to be the user’s main focus. It also mapped well with how users thought about organizing their physical clothing. Each piece of clothing had its own place in a specific drawer or closet. Each drawer contains clothing that all have similar defining attributes.

Main Insights

In building the user flows, I discovered that the user needs for this product fall into three major categories - organizing clothing, managing outfits, and saving inspiration. Thus, clothing, outfits, and inspiration became the basis for the app architecture.

Low Fidelity User Testing

After completing the low fidelity wireframes, I built a Figma prototype and conducted a usability test with three users. All were aged 18-35 and estimated their phone usage per week to be 11-26 hours or higher. The tests uncovered four problematic patterns in the prototype.

Chatbot as homepage

Users were confused about the initial conversation with the chatbot being the homepage. They were uncertain about the site navigation and where they were in the app. To remedy this, I built a typical landing page to introduce users to the application, with a CTA that immediately leads them to try the chatbot.

Displaying too much information

Users felt overwhelmed by the amount of resources the chatbot recommended at one time. I redesigned the flow so that it would recommend content one at a time, and then users have the option to either see more or finish their conversation with the chatbot.

Navigating the Career Guide

Users felt that scrolling through the Career Guide to find content they needed took too long. To solve this issue, I removed the Career Guide sections and focused on emphasizing the latest content. A text menu showing the different topics covered in the Career Guide allows users to reveal more content.

Unclear site navigation

Users found it difficult to find their bookmarked resources due to the unclear site navigation. I initially had it under the Home tab, but two users were uncertain about what would be under Home and thought their resources would be under the Profile icon. I’d incorrectly assumed that users would look under Profile to find their settings and to log out.

To solve this, I reorganized the site navigation. After sign up or login, Home would become Dashboard, making it clearer that various types of information could be accessed from that screen. On the main navigation bar, I changed the Profile icon to a Settings icon to prevent users from looking there for their bookmarked content. Users would instead be able to access their profile information on the Dashboard.

Branding and Identity

Mindmap

To build the Jumpstart brand, I started by brainstorming all the words that come to mind. I then boiled it down to three words that make up the core identity.

Bold. Bold ambitions require bold action. Jumpstart will help you prepare to make all those career moves.

Energetic. Starting your career is a huge endeavor. Jumpstart’s here to give you that starting push.

Supportive. It can be tough getting your foot in the door. Jumpstart’s got your back and will be with you every step of the way

Logo

Jumpstart’s icon is a lightning bolt shaped from two marks. The lightning bolt is symbolic of energy and the idea of jumpstarting one’s career. It’s shaped from two marks to give the impression of building blocks or stairs (with Jumpstart being a good foundation to help one get a start on their career).

Typography

I selected Montserrat as the brand typeface for major headlines and Lato as the brand typeface for all other text. The chatbot typeface is Lato. It’s a modern, readable sans-serif. The semi-rounded details of the characters also give it a warmth that is effective in a chatbot. It engenders trust with users, reinforcing the chatbot’s supportive personality.

Color Palette

I selected bold colors (which you can see in the style guide) that best convey Jumpstart’s personality. Orange is symbolic of vitality and friendliness. It evokes energy. Blue is commonly used to inspire trust and a feeling of security. Users should feel comfortable trusting Jumpstart to help with their careers. Fuschia and violet are vibrant and contribute to building visual interest throughout the app.

Visual Design

High Fidelity Mockups

With the brand identity established, I began to create high fidelity mockups. The majority of Wardrobe’s UI is white and grey in order to allow the uploaded content to be the main focus. Gold and blue are used sparingly throughout the app, in accordance with Wardrobe’s brand guidelines.

I created inviting empty states to encourage guests and new users to try out Wardrobe’s features.

I decided to use navigation bars with large titles to “provide extra emphasis on context”, a recommendation from Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines. The large nav bar text and the bolded tab bar icons help users differentiate between the different areas of content. I also placed extra emphasis on the “Add” button so that the ability to add content is clear from anywhere in the app.

High Fidelity User Testing

After finishing the high-fidelity prototype, I ran another round of usability testing to identify any problems with the visual design. The user tests uncovered three major issues.

Resource Bundle card design

Users didn’t grasp the idea of a Resource Bundle and thought it was the same as other resources recommended by the CareerBot. To solve this, I extended the Jumpstart branding and renamed the Resource Bundle to a Jumpstart Bundle. I gave Jumpstart Bundles an unique icon and redesigned it with stacked cards to reinforce the concept of a bundle.

Interacting with content

Users had difficulty locating the controls to bookmark or share content. My solution was to group the icons on a grey background to make it look more like a toolbar. I also moved it under the page’s title so the user would associate it with the Bundle instead of the chat.

Career Guide menu

Users didn’t recognize that the menu to browse different categories of the Career Guide was a menu of clickable links. My solution was to move the Career Guide menu to the top of the screen to indicate that its the main menu for that page. I gave the menu a grey background to group the links together and distinguish the menu from surrounding whitespace. I bolded the links and used the orange brand color to make it clear that they’re clickable.

Final Product

A virtual closet

Users can upload photos of clothing items they own, saving attributes such as color, brand, and size. They can use their closet to keep track of their clothing inventory and identify items they might need. If they’re out shopping, they can easily check sizes and see what brands they already know they like.

Outfit tagging

Users can track outfits they’ve worn and visualize how their style changes over time. They can also experiment with new outfits by creating outfit collages.

Outfit collaging

Users can create new outfit combinations in an outfit collage. Experiment with their style without having to try clothes on.

Convenient uploads

Users can upload content from anywhere in the app using the tab bar. They can use the camera or conveniently import photos from the web.

Summary

What I learned

Document everything. During this project I kept track of all ideas, decisions, and deliverables in a Design Journal document in Dropbox Paper. I have a tendency to revisit and question crucial decisions. Documenting my rationale helped refresh my memory about certain design decisions and resist the urge to toss everything and start from scratch.

Conversational design. My solution for this project allowed me to learn more about what scenarios chat is appropriate for and what goes into designing natural and thoughtful conversations.

Don’t be afraid to experiment and make mistakes. Sometimes I shy away from experimenting when it comes to visual design because I see it as a more artistic sphere - a muddy unknown that makes me uncomfortable. However, as I received feedback and revised this product’s design, I’ve reinforced two things: 1) there are best practices and guidelines to follow in visual design, and 2) making layouts and using colors that don’t work, and learning why they don’t work, is how I become a better designer.

Generate a solution based on evidence, not intuition. It can be difficult to break out of the user mindset. I’ve tried to solve this by thinking of UX design as the scientific method (I have a science-based background). Have a hypothesis, test it out, and make adjustments based on evidence instead of relying on what I would want to see or how I think I would use the product.

If I had more time to develop Wardrobe further, I would like to test the prototype on mobile so that the test environment would be closer to the end user’s environment. Wardrobe is a mobile app, but because of technology restraints I had to watch users test it on a laptop screen. I’d also like to explore a calendar feature where users can create outfits and plan when they’ll be worn. Finally I’d like to explore the information design of clothing usage statistics such as cost per wear and most worn items.

Going deeper

I’d like to explore other use cases for the chatbot. Perhaps the chatbot could encourage users to explore their options for career counseling in the physical world, by allowing them to schedule calls with real life career counselors.
