Jumpstart

Bridging the career knowledge gap.

It’s common for students and early-career professionals to feel at a loss as to how to build their careers.

Jumpstart is a web app that uses a chatbot to help them navigate their start in the professional world.

The Problem

In the United States, high school students are told to blindly go to college, take out loans, pick majors that they have little concrete knowledge in, and graduate college with a mountain of debt and no idea what to do with their degree. On top of that, not all colleges are created equal. Across the nation, institutions vary widely in the types of resources and connections they can provide. In some institutions the advisement ratio is as poor as 1:1600. How can we bring students the information they need and level the playing field?

The Solution

Jumpstart uses a chatbot to connect users with career development content depending on their level of experience. The chatbot will assess where users are in their careers, learn more about their goals, and then recommend resources from the web and Jumpstart’s Career Guide. The Career Guide is a curated collection of high quality content about career development, including networking, resumes and cover letters, interviewing, salary negotiation, and more.

Research and Discovery

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis on products from three companies in the employment industry: LinkedIn, CareerBuilder, and Indeed. I learned about what each product does well, and more importantly, what they don’t do well.

Each product has job search features and Indeed stands out with the best execution of job search. Indeed also has reviews and profiles of popular companies, which would be useful for job seekers doing research on employers.

LinkedIn is unparalleled in its brand recognition and extensive social networking features. However, its social network proves to be a double-edged sword. Spammers abound in LinkedIn messages and users chasing “influencer” status post well-meaning, but low-value content.

CareerBuilder does a little bit of everything. It has articles on applying for jobs, a directory with information about different career paths, and even a mobile app that displays job listing locations in augmented reality. However, it’s a bloated product that suffers from a confusing user experience.

Read my entire competitive analysis here.

User Survey

I surveyed 46 people on their frustrations with their career development and gained some valuable insight into the problem I’m interested in. I discovered that a substantial minority of participants who were students were not confident in their chances of finding a job upon graduation. Half of participants who were college grads also reported not being confident in their job prospects when they were students.

70% of survey participants reported having felt frustrated with their clothing, agreeing with the statement “I often feel like I have plenty of clothes but nothing to wear”.

79% keep track of their wardrobes mentally or not at all.

81% said they would be interested in trying a digital product that helps them plan and track their wardrobes.

95% would prefer to access such digital wardrobe content on mobile.

User Personas

I interviewed three users who participated in the survey and condensed insights from those interviews to build user personas. Recurring emotions and pain points from the interviews include:

  • feeling like everyone else knows what they’re doing, especially when it comes to in-person networking
  • struggling to make free time, let alone make time to contemplate the future
  • talking to career services in person tends to be a last resort
  • amount of information online is overwhelming

The users tend to fall into three categories:

  • Students early in their education - figuring out their intended majors and career path, very new to college life
  • Students in later stages of their education - might be looking for internships or even their first jobs in their fields
  • Recent grads or career changers

Check out the personas here.

Main Insights

Both students and college grads alike wish they had more access to practical information about the professional world, such as resume building, interview tips, etc. Students also wish they had more in-depth information about the different career paths. College grads wish they had had more information about how to plan classes, internships, and their overall trajectory throughout their college years.

I identified two major opportunities for a new competitor: 1. Curation. There is a glut of career information out there. A significant amount of user survey participants reported finding it difficult to access answers to their career-related questions. How’s a busy person to know what content is worth their time and attention? 2. Entry-level is underserved. Not all job seekers have the same needs, but each of the products I analyzed is built like they do. How can we provide early-career users with a better experience?

Low Fidelity Wireframes

I now understood enough about user goals to begin wireframing what this product might look like. I began by sketching my ideas for Wardrobe’s screens on paper.

I then built digital wireframes with Figma. Throughout the wireframing process I regularly referred back to Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines to make sure I was designing a great user experience for an iOS app.

I used a card-based design because it chunks content for quick scannability and allows the image-based content to be the user’s main focus. It also mapped well with how users thought about organizing their physical clothing. Each piece of clothing had its own place in a specific drawer or closet. Each drawer contains clothing that all have similar defining attributes.

See all the wireframes here.

Main Insights

In building the user flows, I discovered that the user needs for this product fall into three major categories - organizing clothing, managing outfits, and saving inspiration. Thus, clothing, outfits, and inspiration became the basis for the app architecture.

Low Fidelity User Testing

After completing the low fidelity wireframes, I built a Figma prototype and conducted a usability test with three users. All were aged 18-35 and estimated their phone usage per week to be 11-26 hours or higher. The tests uncovered three problematic patterns in the prototype.

Chatbot as homepage

Users were confused about the initial conversation with the chatbot being the homepage. They were uncertain about the site navigation and where they were in the app. To remedy this, I built a typical landing page to introduce users to the application, with a CTA that immediately leads them to try the chatbot.

Displaying too much information

Users felt overwhelmed by the amount of resources the chatbot recommended at one time. I redesigned the flow so that it would recommend content one at a time, and then users have the option to either see more or finish their conversation with the chatbot.

Navigating the Career Guide

Users felt that scrolling through the Career Guide to find content they needed took too long. To solve this issue, I removed the Career Guide sections and focused on emphasizing the latest content. A text menu showing the different topics covered in the Career Guide allows users to reveal more content.

Problem 4

Users struggled to move content between categories because the function to do so was not located where they expected it to be.

I designed the initial wireframes so that users could move an item by accessing the “More options” menu, tapping “Move”, and selecting their desired category.

Half of the users I tested the Wardrobe prototype with were successful in moving an item with the previous flow, so I kept it in the next iteration. However, the other half had expected to be able to move an item after tapping “Edit”, a finding that surprised me.

In the initial wireframes, the Details Editor had no obvious options for moving an item to a different category. Users would have had to save the item, then select a category once prompted. Because this wasn’t clear, no users were able to move an item using this method.

Solution

I redesigned the Details Editor so that users would easily discover multiple paths for moving content.

Branding and Identity

Mindmap

I determined multiple words that should come to mind when one thinks of their wardrobe, and used these words for the basis of the Wardrobe brand. Wardrobe brand targets a younger audience, but should appeal to all those ambitious dreamers looking to achieve their goals and look stylish while doing it. Wardrobe seeks to impart the mindset that style is about more than “just clothes”; it's about self-expression and confidence.

Logo

I sketched multiple concepts for the Wardrobe logo. All were inspired from methods of clothing organization.

I eliminated clothing hooks early on because clothing hooks were less recognizable. Wardrobe’s logo should immediately evoke the idea of clothing or clothing organization. Users I asked for feedback immediately understood hangers and wardrobes, but were uncertain about the clothing hooks.

After another iteration of logos I decided to move forward with the wardrobes, symbolic of the product name. Wardrobes also convey the idea of clothing organization on a macro level more than hangers do.

Typography

I selected Playfair Display as the brand typeface for major headlines and Open Sans as the brand typeface for all other text. This decision is jointly inspired by the elegance of high fashion magazines and a simple modernism that is appropriate for the Web.

The Wardrobe mobile app uses SF Pro Display and SF Pro Text, adhering to Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines for iOS.

Color Palette

The Wardrobe colors are gold and royal blue. Gold is associated with prosperity and success. Royal blue connotes elegance and sophistication, but also trustworthiness. Users should feel all of the mentioned adjectives when thinking of the Wardrobe brand.

Visual Design

High Fidelity Mockups

With the brand identity established, I began to create high fidelity mockups. The majority of Wardrobe’s UI is white and grey in order to allow the uploaded content to be the main focus. Gold and blue are used sparingly throughout the app, in accordance with Wardrobe’s brand guidelines.

I created inviting empty states to encourage guests and new users to try out Wardrobe’s features.

I decided to use navigation bars with large titles to “provide extra emphasis on context”, a recommendation from Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines. The large nav bar text and the bolded tab bar icons help users differentiate between the different areas of content. I also placed extra emphasis on the “Add” button so that the ability to add content is clear from anywhere in the app.

High Fidelity User Testing

Usability Testing

After finishing the high-fidelity prototype, I ran another round of usability testing to identify any problems with the visual design. The user tests uncovered two major issues.

Problem 1

Users didn’t grasp the idea of a Resource Bundle and thought it was the same as other resources recommended by the CareerBot.

Solution

I extended the Jumpstart branding and renamed the Resource Bundle to a Jumpstart Bundle. I gave Jumpstart Bundles an unique icon and redesigned it with stacked cards to reinforce the concept of a bundle.

Problem 2

Users had difficulty locating the controls to bookmark or share content.

Solution

I grouped the icons on a grey background to make it look more like a toolbar. I also moved it under the page’s title so the user would associate it with the Bundle instead of the chat.

Problem 3

Users didn’t recognize that the menu to browse different categories of the Career Guide was a menu of clickable links.

Solution

I moved the Career Guide menu to the top of the screen to indicate that its the main menu for that page. I gave the menu a grey background to group the links together and distinguish the menu from surrounding whitespace. I bolded the links and used the orange brand color to make it clear that they’re clickable.

Final Product

A virtual closet

Users can upload photos of clothing items they own, saving attributes such as color, brand, and size. They can use their closet to keep track of their clothing inventory and identify items they might need. If they’re out shopping, they can easily check sizes and see what brands they already know they like.

Outfit tagging

Users can track outfits they’ve worn and visualize how their style changes over time. They can also experiment with new outfits by creating outfit collages.

Outfit collaging

Users can create new outfit combinations in an outfit collage. Experiment with their style without having to try clothes on.

Convenient uploads

Users can upload content from anywhere in the app using the tab bar. They can use the camera or conveniently import photos from the web.

Summary

What I learned

Document everything. During this project I kept track of all ideas, decisions, and deliverables in a Design Journal document in Dropbox Paper. I have a tendency to revisit and question crucial decisions. Documenting my rationale helped refresh my memory about certain design decisions and resist the urge to toss everything and start from scratch.

Conversational design. My solution for this project allowed me to learn more about what scenarios chat is appropriate for and what goes into designing natural and thoughtful conversations.

Don’t be afraid to experiment and make mistakes. Sometimes I shy away from experimenting when it comes to visual design because I see it as a more artistic sphere - a muddy unknown that makes me uncomfortable. However, as I received feedback and revised this product’s design, I’ve reinforced two things: 1) there are best practices and guidelines to follow in visual design, and 2) making layouts and using colors that don’t work, and learning why they don’t work, is how I become a better designer.

Generate a solution based on evidence, not intuition. It can be difficult to break out of the user mindset. I’ve tried to solve this by thinking of UX design as the scientific method (I have a science-based background). Have a hypothesis, test it out, and make adjustments based on evidence instead of relying on what I would want to see or how I think I would use the product.

If I had more time to develop Wardrobe further, I would like to test the prototype on mobile so that the test environment would be closer to the end user’s environment. Wardrobe is a mobile app, but because of technology restraints I had to watch users test it on a laptop screen. I’d also like to explore a calendar feature where users can create outfits and plan when they’ll be worn. Finally I’d like to explore the information design of clothing usage statistics such as cost per wear and most worn items.

Going deeper

I’d like to explore other use cases for the chatbot. Perhaps the chatbot could encourage users to explore their options for career counseling in the physical world, by allowing them to schedule calls with real life career counselors.
